# The Turing Test

## ELI5
In 1950, before computers were really a thing, Alan Turing asked "can machines think?" — but realized you can't measure "thinking" directly. So he flipped it: set up a game where a machine tries to fool a human judge into believing it's the real human. If the judge can't tell who's who, who cares what's happening inside? You turned a foggy philosophy question into a test you can actually run.

## Core Concept
Alan Turing's 1950 paper asked **"can machines think?"** and, more usefully, *how could you even tell?* His answer is the **imitation game** (the Turing Test): a machine and a human each try to convince a human judge that they are the real human; the machine passes if the judge can't reliably tell them apart.

The test is imperfect and much-criticized, but its lasting significance is that it **grounds a philosophical question in an objective measurement**. That idea of objectivity — benchmarks and measurement — became a dominant driving force in AI progress; it "starts with Turing."

Turing deliberately left the *solution* open. He didn't know how to build it (he gestured at machine learning; others favored logic), but he defined a **North Star** — which is the nature of research: you set the target before you know the path.
