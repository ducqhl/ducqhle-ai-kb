# AI Winters

## ELI5
Twice in history, people got super excited about AI, promised it would solve everything in a few years, then couldn't deliver. When the hype crashed, the money dried up and everyone stopped even saying the word "AI." Those funding-freeze periods are called AI winters — and the pattern (overpromise → underdeliver → funding cut) keeps rhyming.

## Core Concept
Two boom-bust cycles where AI funding collapsed after over-promising:

**First AI winter (after 1966).** The 1950s were high-optimism: Arthur Samuel's checkers program, Newell & Simon's Logic Theorist proving theorems from *Principia Mathematica* (even finding a more elegant proof). People thought AI was a few years away. But problems were framed as **search**, the search space grew exponentially and outpaced 1950s hardware, and systems lacked world knowledge. The folklore machine-translation failure ("the spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak" → "the vodka is good, but the meat is rotten") and a 1966 report led to funding being cut.

**Second AI winter (end of 1980s).** Expert systems (hand-written rules from domain experts) were AI's first real industry impact, but the rules couldn't handle real-world **uncertainty**, became too complex to maintain ("spaghetti code"), and over-hyped/under-delivered — plus economic recession. Funding collapsed again.

Silver lining: the first era still produced lasting tools — **Lisp**, garbage collection, time-sharing. Liang notes the "solve AI in a few years" optimism "sounds a bit familiar" today.
